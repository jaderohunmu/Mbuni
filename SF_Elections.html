Web Redesign

SF Department of Elections Redesigned

We redesigned the SF Department of Elections website using a knowledge-base UI design system and optimized CTAs so that users can take action quickly

  • Role: Product Designer
  • September 2020
  • TEAM: 1 UX Researcher; 3 UX Designers

Overview

Young voters should know how, when, and where to vote in this year's election after visiting the SF Department of Elections website. My team enhanced the SF Department of Elections website by leveraging knowledge-based design patterns, modern icons and introduced a card-based design system to reduce cognitive overload while increasing conversion.


Problem

Research conducted by UNICEF's Global Office of Insights and Policy suggest the following deterrents that negatively impact youth civic behavior: lack of trust in the internet due to high prevalence of false news and misinformation, declining trust in political processes, harassment and trolling, data breaches, and digital surveillance. BUT WAIT! There's more...

American Youth Voting at the Polls

A 2020 study by The New York Times suggest that fewer than half of Americans 18 to 29 voted in the 2016 election

U.S. 5th to Last in Youth Turnout

In a 2020 New York Times study, out of 24 countries sampled, the United States had the fifth-lowest youth turnout

Why Low Voter Turnout? - Dr. Hillygus of "Making Young Voters"

Research suggest that overwhelming registration procedures, as well as a lack of understaning concerning voting procedures and guidelines help contribute toward low turnout


Previous Next
How might the SF Department of Elections, an official government site, help educate young SF voters on guidelines and procedures while instilling the confidence they need to participate in this years election?

Research


User Research & Insights

In addition to our desk research, we conducted virtual interviews, identified key insights, and created our persona. His name is Justin McClain, an aspiring tech worker in his early twenties that lives in the heart of San Francisco. Justin became our archetype that inspired our ideas and design.

. . .

Usability Testing

We conducted five usability tests with users that fit our persona. It's important to note that voters like Justin haven't voted in a general election. As a result, we focused our testing on three critical tasks that would help users successfully participate in this year's election:

Task 1: Register to vote

The Homepage
We would like to see the Register To Vote link above the fold as a main CTA. Young voters who already find this process overwhelming

Registration Basics Page
After selecting the link, users are met with a useful but disorienting page title "registration basics,' but yet expected to begin registering

Registration Link
Research suggest several strengths associated with the use of accordions in mobile design, however the registration link is hidden at first


Previous Next

Task 2: Find nearest polling place

"I Can't Find My Polling Place"
25% of our users could not find their local polling place as key links and features are hidden under content dense accordions

7 Clicks Too Many
This task requires 7 clicks for user's to locate their polling station, resulting in low findability and increased use of the sites search engine function

An Unfimiliar Page
Users were eventually navigated to a new and unfamiliar page to complete a form. Recall, young voters already mistrust government agencies


Task 3: Learn How To Vote by Mail

"Too Busy"
With the Covid-19 pandemic, users were interested in voting by mail, but found the page too busy, despite the valiant accordion

Content Dense Accordions
Content dense accordions overwhelms our young voters and inhibit them from learning about important mail-in ballot procedures

"Ways to Vote" Video Content
Unfortunatly, hidden among the sea of text is a useful 30min video that explains the ways in which people can vote in the 2020 elections

Design


Design Starts With Vision

We put together a moodboard inspired by modern knowledge management systems, help desk UI, and e-learning websites that were easy to navigate with clear CTAs. We relied on the websites native colors and information architecture to ease adoption and feasibility to implement. Lastly we set out to enhance findability by exposing key features while elevating the overall aesthetic for young voters.

Sketches & Low Fi

We quickly drafted sketechs of the mobile site to help set the foundation for our low and mid-fidelity wireframes based on our research, usability studies, and intented vision. This also involved site mapping current and future state user flows

Mid-Fi & Usability Testing

We agreed to use my low-fidelity mockups to conduct initial usability tests using InVision. Once we correct glaring usability issues and gained confidence in the design, we began digitalizing our designs

Scrolling Down The Redesigned Homepage

1. Search Bar
We moved the search bar into the hero section so that users can quickly research their inquiry

2. Register To Vote CTA
We added a "Register To Vote" CTA just below the hero to increase registration conversation

3. Card UI
We used card based design pattern to provide consistency and guide users to core taskd on the website

Learning Ways to Vote Using Exisiting Video Content

Video Content
Research suggest that young people go to sites like YouTube to become more knowledgeable

1. Card Design for Video Content
We leveraged the 30min video the department created, divide the video content into short clips, and featured applicable clips into each "Learn to Vote" sub-page

2. Suggested Videos & FAQs
We re-positioned the exisiting accordion features and corresponding informaiton/FAQs just above the footer if users had additional inquiries

Increased Finadability of User's Polling Place By Aggregating Pages

1. Find Your Polling Place
25% of users in our usability study couldn't find their polling location. We simplified this user flow by including the mapping feature on the page

2. Ballot Return Method Filter
Users can also filter by methods in which they would like to return their ballot (mail, drop off site, and in-person)

3. Procedures & FAQs
We leveraged existing accordion UI so that information and FAQs related to polling procedures might be exposed as needed

Refreshed SF Department of Elections Style Guide

Our goal is to enhance the overall asthetic and findability of the website, but we dont want to stray too far from the native design. As a result, we leveraged the sites exisiting color pallete, some imagery, and mirror the topography to match the SF's city government site

SF Department of Elections Redesigned

Hi-Fidelity Prototype

We redesigned the SF Department of Elections website using a knowledge-base UI design system and optimized CTAs so that users can take action quickly


Homepage


User testing revealed that our mid-fi homepage was still content dense. Despite our modern card design, users continued to find themselves scrolling down the page to execute a task or find the information they need

As a result, we made slight changes to the information architecture on the homepage. To help improve homepage navigation on both mobile and desktop UI we added a secondary navigation bar that divides the content on the homepage into three distinct pages


Find Voting Sites


User research revealed that our mid-fi prototype didn’t effectively display multiple voting locations in one view. For example, there could be multiple ways of voting within a neighborhood (official drop boxes, in-person voting center or polling site, or USPS services)

We designed both a list and map view UI component on our mobile interface which can be accessed using a toggle button. We drew our inspiration from Eater Austin's UI


Ways To Vote


Again, research suggests that young people go to sites like YouTube to become more knowledgeable. We also wanted to diversify how content was being delivered to our users to increase engagement.

We leveraged the 30min video the department created, divide the video content into short clips, and featured applicable clips into each "Learn to Vote" sub-page. We also re-positioned the existing accordion UI and corresponding information/FAQs just above the footer if users had additional inquiries

Lessons Learned

UX blogs often stress the importance of user research when designing a new product or UI. Given the time constraints for this project, our desk research and usability studies provided initial quantitative and qualitative information about our target users needs and behaviors. However, I would have liked to continue to conduct additional research to learn more about our target users by:


Managing our project schedule to allow more time to address the sites IA, especially for websites that are heavy in content, tools, and services

Designing studies to learn more about how our users would have organized content structured on the website using card sorting exercises

Conducting A/B test with users to compare alternative designs and measure differences in task performance as opposed to visual preferences
