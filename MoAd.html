Web Redesign

MoAD - Museum of The African Diaspora (San Francisco)

We created an immersive digital experience for creators and MoAd viewers to facilitate cultural dialogue and learning through the global lens of the African Diaspora

  • Role: Product Designer
  • July 2020
  • TEAM: 1 UX Researcher; 3 UX Designers

Overview

What a time to be alive! With museum closures around the country due to COVID-19, MoAD has vastly increased in online programming and wanted to develop a new and exciting digital voice connecting artists to its audiences in a more meaningful way. As a team of eager designers (and frequent patrons of the Museum), we wanted to assist MoAD in its quest to reinvigorate and excite its digital presence.


Business Problem

To being our redesign journey, we first reached out to the MoADs Digital Director to better understand the business objectives MoAD had set for their website. Mark and our team had envisioned three core areas to improve MoADs digital experience:

More Than a Museum...

Mark wanted to create a space for the MoAd community to share, view, discuss, and even purchase art/content

Difficult Navigation...

MoAD’s top navigation architecture includes 10+ different elements structured as a double navigation bar

Underwhelming UI...

MoAD’s web experience was due for a UX and visual overhaul given its outdated design

Research


Research Methods & Findings

After aligning our objectives with our stakeholders, we developed proto-personas to begin to think about the needs and goals of MoAD's community. We then conducted user research to try and validate our proto-persona through user interviews, qualitative surveys, and analysis of both direct and indirect competitors.

Interviews

All 5 interviewers shared the following sentiments:

“I struggle to find both online content and entertainment that spurs inspiration and feeds their curiosity about the world, cultures, art and history”

Qualitative Survey

20 survey questions, 50 particioants responded

85% of survey respondents visit museums websites to learn about exhibits and events

85% haven't visited museums websites during COVID

Competitor Analysis

4 key direct and indirect competitors

Vocal and Medium provided insights into business models that allow creators and artists to generate revenue through online communities


In addition, the contemporary art market in Ivory Coast and across sub-Saharan Africa has captured collectors' imaginations on a global scale, translating into profits for many artists like Saint-Etienne. With this amazing opportunity in front of us we refined our "how might we" statement...

How might MoAD's digital experience become a platform for contemporary artists of the diaspora and a young generation of consumers who realize an emerging scene, story, and new standard?

User Persona

After our research we formed pivotal insights that shaped our persona. Her name is Kierra J., an aspiring creator seeking to create content, share art, and participate within communities interested in Afro/Diaspora-inspired art.

Usability Testing

With a strong understanding of our users' motivation through our persona above and MoAD's vision, we wanted to assess MoADs current usability to uncover existing pain points and opportunities that will help mold the website's future design. We paid particular attention to 3 key organisms that make up the website's framework; the navigation bar, body content, and the footer.

1. Navigation Bar
Currently, MoAD’s top navigation architecture includes 10+ different elements structured as a double navigation bar.

Not only does the top navigation menu (and sub-menu) not meet color accessibility standards. We found that 75% of users tested could not find the calendar link without guidance.

2. Body Content
Currently, MoAD uses a simple card design for its featured online events and content. Card section headers present different font-weight and format styles.

We also found accessibility issues concerning the light purple text on top of a white background and unnecessary white space, making the page seem unstructured and inconsistent.

3. Footer
MoAD’s footer presents accessibility issues concerning the text and background colors. Both the light gray and dark yellow text on its dark gray background do not pass AA standards.

Why not create more contrast for all elements within the footer and move less essential tasks from the 10-item top navigation architecture down to the bottom of the page

Information Architecture

Currently, MoAD’s top navigation architecture includes 10+ different elements structured as a double navigation bar. How might we simplify the navigation architecture so that it’s clear, highlights important site destinations, and meets accessibility standards?

Current IA
We wanted to know how our users would categorize these pages without context. The website's current taxonomy included language that may or may not be similar to those visiting for the first time.

Card Sorting Activity
Using OptimalSort + Zoom, we chose to conduct a hybrid card sorting activity with 6 participants. Participants were provided with a predetermined set of category elements and were allowed to create new categories when appropriate.



Design Starts with Vision

We derived our design and mvp features by crafting a mood board inspired by direct and indirect competitors. We also retooled our navigation architecture and added a new service to MoAd's business model to expand their digital experience.

Our Mood-Board was inspired by...

1. Modern museum design systems from sites like The Walker and SFMOMA, 2. Business models used on online communities like DeviantArt and Medium that help artists get exposure and generate income, and 3. Grid layout patterns on online magazines like Artnet and The New Yorker to display articles and content

Information Architecture + New Feature

And with feedback from our card sorting exercise, we simplified the navigation architecture and added a new feature called "MoAd+." MoAd+ is a subscription service that connects featured artists and consumers to share, discuss, and purchase content from creatives of the diaspora.

Sketches & Low Fi

We opted to design for mobile first before creating a desktop version in accordance to recent product design principles. This allowed us to focus on core website features given the limitations preset on mobile interfaces.

Mid-Fi: MoAD's Long-Scrolling Homepage

1. Homepage Top Navigation
To address a busy navigation bar, we simplified the top navigation bar using insights gained through our cart-sorting exercise and reduced the number of menu options by 33%.

To spotlight MoADs creators and their content, we built a featured section just beneath the fold. Our goal is to empower visitors to engage in a virtual dialog with content creators as they search and navigate the site. Based on searches and clicks, MoAD will display contextually relevant content that matches the interests of the visitors.

2. Long-Scrolling Homepage
Currently, MoAD uses a simple card design for its featured online events, exhibitions, and content. We kept this native design pattern but modernized its aesthetic by rounding image corners while using consistent font-weights and format styles.

Consistent with magazine-inspired UI, we surfaced additional content on the homepage for users to scroll through while visiting MoAD's website. The long-scrolling page keeps users entertained, engaged and increases the likelihood for users to react to calls to action.

3. Dark or Low Light Theme
Lastly, we opted for a dark or low-light theme for several reasons. First, we wanted a theme that was consistent with the context of those physically visiting the Museum. Many events take place in the evening at San Francisco's South Market District.

Second, the dark theme also creates a more dramatic aesthetic that emboldens the visual art and exhibitions found at MoAD.


Mid-Fi: MoAD+ Homepage (After users have subscribed)

1. Sticky Sidebar Navigation
After a user subscribes to MoAD+, they will see a UI that mirrors the homepage and side menu that guides users to other types of actionable tasks on the page. In this case, users can create a post within the MoAD+ community, follow suggested creators, or find other content based on their interests.

2. Increase Conversion of Key CTAs
Given the page's long-scrolling design, MoAD benefits from having this friendly and intuitive navigation. Studies have shown that implementing sticky navigation on an e-commerce site can increase conversion for critical calls to action.


Mid-Fi: MoAD Exhibition / Creator Page

1. Hero Banner
We designed a full-screen hero banner to emphasize the featured exhibition and creator. This approach spotlights either the artist, their personality, or their art.

2. Sticky Side Mennu (Again)
Users will also see a sticky side-navigation menu that encourages critical calls to action. Users can follow or subscribe to the creator's page, tip the creator for their work (revenue stream), share the page, and view similar creators on MoAD+.

3. THE VALUE PROPOSITION
We replaced the existing Carousels/Slider hero with a gallery view. Each artist's gallery item is interactive. It allows users to learn about each item in the exhibit when clicked, interact with the MoAD+ community, engage with the artist, and purchase their art.

In addition, the secondary navigation menu allows users to navigate the Artist or Exhibition's content (post, gallery, items for sale, bio) in a way they can control.

Hi-Fidelity Prototype

We redesigned the SF Department of Elections website using a knowledge-base UI design system and optimized CTAs so that users can take action quickly


Homepage


User testing revealed that our mid-fi homepage was still content dense. Despite our modern card design, users continued to find themselves scrolling down the page to execute a task or find the information they need

As a result, we made slight changes to the information architecture on the homepage. To help improve homepage navigation on both mobile and desktop UI we added a secondary navigation bar that divides the content on the homepage into three distinct pages


Find Voting Sites


User research revealed that our mid-fi prototype didn’t effectively display multiple voting locations in one view. For example, there could be multiple ways of voting within a neighborhood (official drop boxes, in-person voting center or polling site, or USPS services)

We designed both a list and map view UI component on our mobile interface which can be accessed using a toggle button. We drew our inspiration from Eater Austin's UI


Ways To Vote


Again, research suggests that young people go to sites like YouTube to become more knowledgeable. We also wanted to diversify how content was being delivered to our users to increase engagement.

We leveraged the 30min video the department created, divide the video content into short clips, and featured applicable clips into each "Learn to Vote" sub-page. We also re-positioned the existing accordion UI and corresponding information/FAQs just above the footer if users had additional inquiries

Lessons Learned

UX blogs often stress the importance of user research when designing a new product or UI. Given the time constraints for this project, our desk research and usability studies provided initial quantitative and qualitative information about our target users needs and behaviors. However, I would have liked to continue to conduct additional research to learn more about our target users by:


Managing our project schedule to allow more time to address the sites IA, especially for websites that are heavy in content, tools, and services

Designing studies to learn more about how our users would have organized content structured on the website using card sorting exercises

Conducting A/B test with users to compare alternative designs and measure differences in task performance as opposed to visual preferences
