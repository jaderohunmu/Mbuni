SF Department of Elections Redesigned

We redesigned the SF Department of Elections website using a knowledge-base UI design system and optimized CTAs so that users can take action quickly

Overview

Young voters should know how, when, and where to vote in this year's election after visiting the SF Department of Elections website. However, my team uncovered several usability issues that made the site hard to navigate, busy, and time-consuming. As a result, we enhanced the website by leveraging knowledge-based design patterns to inform young people about voting in San Francisco. Lastly, we added modern icons and introduced a card-based design system to reduce cognitive overload while increasing conversion where appropriate

Role

Lead UX Designer & Project Manager
Responsible for wireframing, prototyping, strategy, and project management

Team

The Problem


We're amid a pandemic, we are fighting for black lives, employment is hard to find, and it's an election year. With everything going, voting matters now more than ever. However, SF news channel ABC7 conducted an analysis of voter registration data that suggested only 42 percent of young California voters ages 18 to 24 cast their vote in the 2016 presidential election

We believe that the SF Department of Elections can be a great resource to help engage SF youth during local and national election cycles. As a result we want to address the SF Department of Elections dense content and inconsistent UI design system, making it it easy for people, especially SF youth, to find ways to vote safely during these unusual times

Research conducted by UNICEF's Global Office of Insights and Policy suggest the following deterrents that negatively impact youth civic behavior: lack of trust in the internet due to high prevalence of false news and misinformation, declining trust in political processes, harassment and trolling, data breaches, and digital surveillance. Young San Fransicans & SF Department of Elections


So what do we know so far? Most eligible young voters in the U.S. don't cast their ballot, especially in California, where only 42 percent voted in the 2016 presidential election. Young people want to vote but they 1) don’t understand nor trust the political process, 2) don’t trust the internet due to fake news and misinformation, and 3) are understandably overwhelmed by it all.

How might the SF Department of Elections, an official government site, help educate young SF voters on guidelines and procedures while instilling the confidence they need to participate in this years election?

The Vision


In our moodboard we were inspired by modern knowledge management systems, help desk UI, and e-learning websites that were easy to navigate with clear CTAs. We relied on the websites native colors and information architecture to ease adoption and feasibility to implement. Lastly we set out to enhance findability by exposing key features while elevating the overall aesthetic for young voters.

After research, testing, and brainstorming we identified 3 areas of opportunity and began to solution our website redesign:





1. Mobile First
Prioritize important content and navigation by applying a mobile design strategy


2. Call To Action
Improve polling site findability by exposing key CTAs on the homepage for users to find quickly


3. White-Space
Reduce cognitive overload by using more white-space to direct attention to the CTA

SF Department of Elections Redesigned

We redesigned the SF Department of Elections website using a knowledge-base UI design system and optimized CTAs so that users can take action quickly






Homepage


Insight

User testing revealed that our mid-fi homepage was still content dense. Content included news, highlights, announcements, Twitter, and several CTAs. Despite our modern card design, users continued to find themselves scrolling down the page to execute a task or find the information they need

Solution

As a result, we made slight changes to the information architecture on the homepage. To help improve homepage navigation on both mobile and desktop UI we adding a secondary navigation bar that divides the content on the homepage into three distinct pages.

Sub-pages include "I want to" for core task, News, and Featured Publications which includes frequently viewed or "Liked" articles published by SF Dept. of Elections regarding election guidelines and procedures





Find Voting Sites


Insight

User research revealed that our mid-fi prototype didn’t effectively display multiple voting locations in one view. For example, there could be multiple ways of voting within a neighborhood (official drop boxes, in-person voting center or polling site, or USPS services)

Solution

We designed both a list and map view UI component on our mobile interface which can be accessed using a toggle button. We see this feature commonly used in popular apps like Airbnb or Google Maps. We drew our inspiration from Eater Austin's List/Map UI





Ways To Vote


Insight

Again, research suggests that young people go to sites like YouTube to become more knowledgeable. We also wanted to diversify how content was being delivered to our users to increase engagement.

Solution

We leveraged the 30min video the department created, divide the video content into short clips, and featured applicable clips into each "Learn to Vote" sub-page. We also re-positioned the existing accordion UI and corresponding information/FAQs just above the footer if users had additional inquiries


Lesson Learned


UX blogs often stress the importance of user research when designing a new product or UI. Given the time constraints for this project, our desk research and usability studies provided initial quantitative and qualitative information about our target users needs and behaviors. However, I would have liked to continue to conduct additional research to learn more about our target users by:



Designing studies to learn more about how our users would have organized content structured on the website using card sorting exercises

Managing our project schedule to allow more time to address the sites IA, especially for websites that are heavy in content, tools, and services

Conducting A/B test with users to compare alternative designs and measure differences in task performance as opposed to visual preferences
