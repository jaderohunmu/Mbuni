CivE - A Civic Engagement App

A platform for people who struggle to find the time, resources, and information needed to effectively participate in civic activities

Overview

Recent political science research suggests that our democracy is being threatened by partisan politics, civil unrest, noisy social media, and declining civic engagement, especially among young people. I led a team that designed an app for young citizens tired of today's social-political climate who struggle to find the time, resources, and information needed to effectively participate in civic activities in their community

CivE is a mobile application that organizes civic activities into actionable, consumable, and interactive modules/bites/pages. We believe CivE will encourage youth to easily find, learn, and participate in civic behaviors needed to create change in their communities.

The Problem

In a 2018 article, by the Center for American Progress, civic knowledge and public engagement is at an all-time low… Not surprisingly, public trust in government is at only 18 percent and prior to the 2020 November election, voter participation had reached its lowest point since 1996.

This is particularly true for Gen Z and millennials. Youth participate when they have opportunities and are asked, but we find that such opportunities are missing in many communities. We call places where civic engagement is absent, civic deserts.

Why Civic Engagement?



Against the backdrop of weakened civic engagement, the issues facing our globalized world are becoming more complexm and the activities that traditionally boost citizens' understanding of these issues—like newspaper readership—are also in decline. Protest and rallys go without legislation for change, and elected officials continue to pretend to representive community interest.

Young People Are Not Civically Engaged

Not surprisingly, Men and Women below the age of 30 engage in less civic activities compared to their senior counterparts

Why? They Dont Know Enough

According the to the PRRI report, 49% of young Americans say they do not know enough about the issues to get involved



But Yet, There's Hope for Democracy

Despite Americans pessimism at the national level, more Americans feel confident that people in their community can bridge political divides to solve local problems. We believe that there is a need to expose alternative access points to civic activities that will promote change and transparency, especially at the local level

User Research

The desk research helped frame research objectives to understand why young voters were less engaged in civic behaviors and activities. To uncover additional insights, we conducted research interviews with users that fit our proto-persona, young early careerist seeking first-time employment who has supported social causes such as BLM



My research encompassed:

Understanding most effective ways in which young voters perceive they can effect change and engage civically in their community

Understanding what barriers/reasons prevent bay area residents from participating in civic behaviors beyond protest and social media post

Qualitative Interviews

Despite our three week timeline for completion, limited availability of users for research given the pandemic, and novice UX researchers available to our team, we virtually interviewed ten people, aged between 20-26 years with diverse backgrounds, to collect qualitative data during discovery

Insights

After conducting 10 user interviews, we built an affinity map to synthesize pain points and opportunities identified. We summarized the data collected into these 3 insights which inspired our platforms core features


Insight #1

As evident through their participation in protests and marches, our respondents want to see positive change occur in their community yet feel discouraged by their government's ability to implement change. They are unaware of the institutions, organizations, and officials likely to influence policy changes locally nor understand how to effectively communicate their policy needs
How might we create more awareness concerning those institutions, organizations, and officials that directly influence policy?

Insight #2

Young people believe voting is the most impactful way to enact change but do not know which officials to vote for given their interest or what measures to support, especially at the local level. Our respondents lack time and stamina to stay engaged civically due to conflicts with other life priorities such as job search, career aspirations, and dating, social life. Young people also fear criticism and conflict with family, friends, work colleagues that might maintain different opinions
How might we make it effortless to learn about and privately support measures and officials before we vote?

Insight #3

The users we interviewed were more likely to engage in civic activities and behaviors while attending college, high-school, or when participating in religious communities. These institutions organized and required civic activities as compulsory for admission or membership. Since leaving these institutions, opportunities to engage in civic action appear less accessible if not hard to find. Our users now rely on social media, petitioners, and influencers within our communities to inform and guide their civic or political activity
How might we organize civic activities and events based on users’ interests, community, and location?

The Vision

Civic participation in America is essential to sustaining our democratic form of government and its supporting institutions. We wanted to create an unbiased, easily consumable, and actionable environment that encourages civic participation at the local level, in order to address the changes young people want to see within their communities

We were able to brainstorm, prioritize, workflow key MVP features inspired by insights gathered from primary research, secondary research, and competitors (both direct and indirect) such as Civic Rush, My America, and Instagram
