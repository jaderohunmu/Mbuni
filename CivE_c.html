CivE | The Civic Engagement App

A platform for people who struggle to find the time, resources, and information needed to effectively participate in civic activities

Overview

Recent political science research suggests that our democracy is being threatened by partisan politics, civil unrest, noisy social media, and declining civic engagement, especially among young people. I led a team that designed an app for young citizens tired of today's social-political climate who struggle to find the time, resources, and information needed to effectively participate in civic activities in their community

CivE is a mobile application that organizes civic activities into actionable, consumable, and interactive pages. We believe CivE will encourage youth to easily find, learn, and participate in civic behaviors needed to create change in their communities.

The Problem


In a 2018 article by the Center for American Progress, civic knowledge and public engagement is suggested to be at an all-time low. Not surprisingly, public trust in government was only 18 percent and voter participation had reached its lowest point since 1996 (prior to November 2020 elections).

In our research we also found that engagement is particularly low for Gen Z and millennials. Youth participate when they have opportunities and are asked, but we find that such opportunities are missing in many communities

Why Civic Engagement?


Against the backdrop of weakened civic engagement, the issues facing our globalized world are becoming more complex and the activities that traditionally boost citizens' understanding of these issues—like newspaper readership—are also in decline.

Protest and rallys go without legislation for change, and elected officials continue to pretend to representive community interest. Young people want to participate civics when they have opportunities and are asked, but such opportunities are missing in many communities.

Young People Are Not Civically Engaged

Not surprisingly, Men and Women below the age of 30 engage in less civic activities compared to their senior counterparts

Why? They Dont Know Enough

According the to the PRRI report, 49% of young Americans say they do not know enough about the issues to get involved



But Yet, There's Hope for Democracy


Despite Americans pessimism at the national level, more Americans feel confident that people in their community can bridge political divides to solve local problems. In addition, research suggest that young people who engage in digital participatory politics are much more likely to engage in ‘real’ offline political participation such as voting.

We believe that there is a need to expose alternative access points to civic activities that will promote change and transparency, especially at the local level

User Research


The desk research helped frame research objectives to understand why young voters were less engaged in civic behaviors and activities. To uncover additional insights, we conducted research interviews with users that fit our proto-persona; newly-graduated early-careerist in thier 20s, seeking first-time employment who support social causes such as BLM



My research encompassed:

Understanding most effective ways in which young voters perceive they can effect change and engage civically in their community

Understanding what barriers/reasons prevent bay area residents from participating in civics beyond protest and social media post

Qualitative Interviews

We virtually interviewed ten people, aged between 20-26 years with diverse backgrounds, to collect qualitative data to understand challenges that prevent civic participation in their community

Insights


After conducting 10 user interviews, we built an affinity map to synthesize pain points and opportunities identified. We summarized the data collected into these 3 insights which inspired our platforms core features


Insight #1

Young people want to see change in their community but feel discouraged by their government's ability to implement change. They are unaware of the institutions, organizations, and officials likely to influence policy changes locally nor understand how to effectively communicate their policy needs

How might we create more awareness concerning those institutions, organizations, and officials that directly influence policy?

Insight #2

Young people believe voting is the most impactful way to enact change but do not know which officials to vote adn what measures to support because they lack time and stamina to enact these civic behaviors. Career growth and social standing seem to take priority over civic participation beyond presidential elections

How might we make it effortless to learn about and privately support measures and officials before we vote?

Insight #3

Young people that attended college, high-school, or religious communities participated in compulsory civic activities to secure admission or membership. Since leaving these institutions, opportunities to engage in civics appear less accessible. These users rely on social media and influencers to guide civic decision-making

How might we organize civic activities and events based on users’ interests, community, and location?

The Vision


Digital platforms provides a low-barrier-to-entry opportunity for young people to create, view, and share civic-related content at potentially massive scale. We wanted to create an unbiased, easily consumable, and actionable environment that encourages civic participation at the local level

We were able to brainstorm, prioritize, workflow key MVP features inspired by insights gathered from primary research, secondary research, and competitors (both direct and indirect) such as Civic Rush, My America, and Instagram

MVP Features & User Flow


Civic Events & Update Feed
Find events in your neighborhood with an easy starting point to engage with your community

VoteOn - Local Measures
Bookmark, share, and take action on proposed measures and legislation in your community

MyGov - Elected Officials
Learn more about the track record of your local representative regarding issues you care about

CivE's Onboarding User Flow


Onboarding Goal
During onboarding, users can curate civic activities, measures, and events on thier home feed by selecting civic issues that are important to the user

Progress Step UI
We included progress step ui controls using both icons and lables to help users know where they are in the onboarding process

Selector Buttons
We designed basic selectors (or contained buttons) to give users a better idea of activities, events, topics they would like featured on their feed



MyGov - Profile Page For Local Leaders & Elected Officials


We designed a profile page showcasing the of users' house representative based on the users home location, creating more awareness of key decision makers that directly influence policy in the users community.

We initially segmented the profile page into four parts: Voting Record/History, Endorsements, Twitter Feed, and Bio. We used card UI to display relevant informaiton for each segment.



VoteOn - Digital Voter Guidebook To Inform Users


Now users can use the app as a virtual voting guide with to better understand measures and proposals that impact their community.

Card UI Design
We used card UI to display ballot information (similar to a voter guidebook) to help users quicly scan, navigate, and bookmark ballot measures for reference.

Rating Controls
In our early designs, users could rate or express support for a measure using rating controls. Rating results were meant to appear after a control was selected so that users can follow results.



CivE - The Civic Engagement App

After 2 rounds of usability test, we were able to to design CivE, a platform that organizes civic activities into an easily consumable, informative, and actionable newsfeed






Onboarding


Input nickname/zip code to locate events, measures, and representatives

Our onboarding page includes short, concise input field labels that are easy to read and scan. Upon completion, the interface displays the user's congressional district representing a small, yet key engagement milestone

Select civic interest to customize your home feed

Testing revealed that users were confused about why they needed to select specific interest while onbboarding. As a result, we added a brief description informing users how their selection curated content on their home feed

Set up notification preferences and accessibility settings

We designed notifications and accessibility settings (language, text size, and dark mode) to ensure our platform is accessible and inclusive for users with color blindness, vision impairments, and language preferences





Home Feed & Events


Sort & Filter UI Patterns

We designed a simple sort-control and modal window that allows users to select civic content based on community activity. Users can also filter content based on community meetings, local rallies, measures, and volunteer events

Card-Based UI

We used card-based UI to organize civic activites within the user's community. Each card links to information pertinent to the civic event/activity and displays community leaders hosting the activity

List View & Calendar View

After users select the "Save" button within an event card, the micro-interaction keeps the user informed by confirming the saved event and guiding users to view the event in their calendar





VoteOn


VoteOn Feature

Initially VoteOn allowed users to react, share, save, and share local ballot measures. During initial testing, users didn't know if they were officially voting while using the Like/Don't Like rating controls. As a result we removed the "Like" rating control altogether to avoid confusion

Reaction Emojis > Rating Controls

We tapped into Jakob's law and utilized emojis to give users an opportunity to react to a measure as opposed to the rating approach outlined in our mid-fi. We took inspiration found on apps like Instagram, Slack, and Facebook

No More Voter Guidebooks

After saving a measure, the interaction is automatically bookmarked for the user for future reference. In addition, each card can collapse using a micro-interaction that eases vertical scrolling





MyGov


Local Representatives AND Community Leaders

MyGov features the user's local House Representative information. However, testing revealed that users also wanted to know other local decision-makers (ex. City Mayor and City Council Members). We added a filter that allows users to select local community leaders of interest

What Legislation Did My Elected Offical Support?

Just below the fold, we designed clickable cards that briefly summarize legislation your elected official did or did not support during their term. Each card opens a modal that provides additional information about the legislation

Elected Officials Tweet Too

Twitter has become a key platform in getting to know our elected officials (for better and for worse). Users might get to know their elected official a bit better by accessing their tweets using a Twitter feed located on the official's profile page


Lesson Learned


I've grown tired of chanting the names of people that should be alive. I'm tired of elected officials that don't look like me but represent my interest. Yet, while I might be tired, this project reminded me that civic engagement is instrumental in sustaining our great democracy. So while we might be tired of it all, we need to know who our local politicians are, we won't stop chanting, and we will hold our elected officials accountable so that they represent our collective interest



As I step down from my soapbox, we also took away the following from this project:

Secondary research was instrumental in leading user research and design

Frequent testing and existing UI patterns informed decision making at every step

Teamwork and constant communication driven by clear roles and expectations
